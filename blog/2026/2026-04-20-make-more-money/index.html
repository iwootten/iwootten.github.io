Investing vs Saving Doubled My Returns on Company Cash

Here’s a minimal effort switch I made that added a couple of thousand pounds both to my UK Company’s profits and personal accounts last year. At a time when nobody knows what’s the hell is going to happen in the world it provided me with some much needed security.

None of what follows should be counted as financial advice, I have no qualifactions for that - it’s just what’s worked for me.

Putting Business Cash to Work

It took me some time to realise when I first started my business in 2012 that it’s completely legitimate for it to be earning interest on the spare cash it had sitting in it’s bank account. I know, I’m an idiot - but to be fair interest rates were so low when I started back then I didn’t care too much. My bank had provided me with a savings account, I just never used the thing. After getting a feel for what my cash flow was going to be over the course of a few years, I started using it and getting a little interest. Never really a lot, but nice to have nonetheless. That was up until the pandemic hit. When the Bank of England base rate rose to finally reach 5.25% in 2023 and banks began passing some of that on it soon amounted to a nice chunk extra each year.

Those were the good old days though and rates that high are long gone. My business bank has gradually reduced that down to 2.25% this year which would be annoying if I were still using it.

Investing in Money Market Funds

As I was slow to taking up saving with my business cash, I’ve been similarly slow to take to investing it. Also completely legitimate and more accessible now here in the UK to smaller companies. I’m not doing anything crazy, I’m using extremely low risk money market funds.

A money market fund is a short-term collection of debt securities and offers slightly higher returns than a savings account. Using them, I was able to achieve 4.45% on my uninvested limited company cash via investengine in the 25/26 year, almost double what my bank would offer via easy access savings. Depending on the size of the sum invested, that could amount to a decent chunk of additional change. Crucially, it’s still accessible at any point in the year for me to withdraw from with its increased value, unlike fixed term products.

Here’s a graph of it’s returns in the past year:

As you can see, up and to the right without much volatility at all over the year - just what I wanted for my business. The ETF I used has ticker CSH2 and is from Amundi with an expense ratio of 0.1%. You can also see an alternative view of it’s returns on justetf.com. Other investment securities are obviously available, though carry far more risk and were unsuitable for me since I knew I would draw on the capital within a year.

The rate you get is likely to be very close to the SONIA rate (Sterling Overnight Index Average) benchmark so take a look at that to get an idea of what it might be currently. SONIA is what banks use to determine what lending rates should be between them.

I found it super simple to do this so long as you leave enough in your bank account to cope with the day to day of your business activities. I’ve had to learn to plan slightly better since it takes a few days to sell and withdraw invested funds to my current account. There’s no additional fees with investengine and your money is FSCS protected to £120k. It’s not going to be for everyone since it’s also going to add some complexity to your accounts if you go this route.

As a nice plus, if you use my link and fund an account we’ll both get a bonus added between £20-£200 for personal and £100-£400 for business accounts.

Whatever you decide be sure to review the returns your getting on cash and see if it is working as best it could for you.
